Wilde's Vision

Oscar Wilde wrote scandalous unsigned pieces as a journalist for the fashion magazine, A Woman’s World. Wilde rebelled against the industrial revolution and its restrictive fashions with flowing locks and velvet suits.

"Fashion is ephemeral. Art is eternal. Indeed what is a fashion really? A fashion is merely a form of ugliness so absolutely unbearable that we have to alter it every six months!"

While offering sympathies with the self-inflicted torture of fashions, he gave his chronicles and counsels, while approving on forms the subject took. To him, it was essential to the Mundus Muliebris of the day, and someone needed to be factful about it.

On mutilation and misery by corsets- Oscar commented on the pity of a civilized woman hanging onto a cross-bar, while her maid laced and packed her up into a 15-inch waist. It was vastly erroneous to imagine that a small circumference gave an air of grace to the figure, and to exaggerate the width of the shoulders and the hips. A Parisian woman wore her waist as a decoration.
Corsets were required to hold the lower garments, but the mistake lay in not suspending all apparel from the shoulders. The corset would then become irrelevant, and the body would be free for motion and respiration.

On high heels- Wilde admitted that though some additional height of the shoe is necessary when wearing long skirts on muddy streets, must that height be given only to heels? Such high heels throw the bodyweight forward, shorten the steps and are wanting of grace. He re-imagined the ancient clogs, sparing away heaviness and height. He dared to re-imagine clogs from times of Henry VI, without too much heaviness or height, and imagined their use as we today use slip-ons.

On hats- What one wore on the extremities of the body, such as head and feet, should be made from a soft, comfortable material. For freedom, it should take its shape from one’s choice, and not from a stiff material. He mocked the thin-brimmed, high-crowned, ‘chimney- pot’ hat that stood tall and refused its owner protection from the sun.

On Layering- In a cold climate, Londoners wore many articles of clothing, all from mistaken materials. Wool was relatively in experiments. A play between the light and lines in rich and rippling folds of garments would add more grace than bows and flounces that were usual to decoration.

A wide-turned down collar is healthier than strangling stock, a cloak more comfortable than a sleeved overcoat- easy to take off, rests lightly on shoulders and makes perfectly cosy in the winters. The doublet jacket still stands preferable to coat & waistcoat for protecting the chest better from cough. The critic made suggestions to slit the overcoat at each side if it went below the waist, to free the legs for movement. Those that have dared removed the slits from Indian kurtas unless they made them Anarkalis have been highly erroneous in judgment.

Wilde appealed to the European man to shun away tight leggings inhibiting circulation of blood, and choose short loose trousers instead. They would stay until Edward, the Prince of Wales, set the tone for the trousers in the modern era. Edward introduced trouser cuffs to lift the trouser hem above the dirt and popularized trouser creases.

The people who made such garments, he said, were taught not in academies but from what they looked at. The delightful proportion in the dress of the Greeks was echoed extensively in their architecture. "A nation arrayed in stove-pipe hats and dress-improvers might have built the Pantechnichon possibly, but the Parthenon never!"

The Victorians preferred to wear monstrosities for beauty and shun comfort to plainness. He articulated this phenomenon, "The ugly thing is merely a thing that is badly made, or a thing that does not deserve its purpose; it is want of fitness; it is failure; it is uselessness, such as an ornament in the wrong place. Beauty is the purgation of all superfluities. There is a divine economy about beauty; it gives us just what is needed and no more, whereas ugliness is always extravagant. Ugliness is spendthrift and wastes its material."

These chronicles give us an idea into the mind of a literary genius and his thoughts on the garments. While we think ourselves free from these tortures as our loose tees finally hang from our shoulders, we have not looked at dress close enough to test its toxic tints. In clothing designed for appearance rather than performance, unconsciously cheap fast-fashion knockoffs allow us to satisfy our desire for a consistently new wardrobe without straining our wallets.

And how desirable are they? To make a market for minimalist millennials cravings, manufacturers choose bright colours, metallic finishes, hard-holding glue that may be very toxic to factory workers. In filling our wardrobes with deliberately disposable clothing, we have unwittingly caused pain, suffering and sometimes even death.
